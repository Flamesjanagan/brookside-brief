Markets have an unusually pure narrative today: a single company reporting after a single bell will signal whether the defining investment theme of the decade is intact. But the quieter, more important story is in the bond market, where the 30-year just hit a level unseen in nineteen years and fund managers are openly discussing 6%.

What to watch

Powell opens the Atlanta Fed conference at 9:00 AM — his first remarks since the hot CPI/PPI prints. Tomorrow's FOMC minutes (2:00 PM) are the bigger event, revealing how deep the four-way dissent ran. The German 10-year Bund climbed to 2.78% in sympathy, confirming this is a global repricing, not a U.S.-only story.

Inflation read The April prints reignited the inflation fear: CPI at +3.8% Y/Y and PPI at a hot +6.0% Y/Y, much of it energy-driven. A BofA fund manager survey out this morning shows 62% expect the 30-year yield to eventually reach 6% — a level last seen in 1999.

The mechanism

The market is desperate for signal on whether the Committee views the energy-driven surge as transitory or structural. With the long bond already at a 19-year high, the bond market is effectively front-running a hawkish read. The equity risk premium has compressed to its thinnest since 2002 as a result.

Growth read The hard data is still resilient. Roughly 95% of the S&P 500 has reported Q1 with blended earnings growth of +11.4% and 76% beating on EPS. The rate-sensitive consumer is bending but not breaking — Lowe's and Home Depot both beat. Friday's flash PMIs are the first real test of whether the rate and energy shock is finally crimping growth.

The pivot point

A genuine growth scare is the one development that would reliably reverse the relentless bond selloff — which is why Friday's flash PMIs and any softening in the labor data suddenly matter more than they have in months. Walmart on Thursday closes the consumer trifecta (consensus $0.66 / $171B).

Risk read Event risk is concentrated. The VIX jumped 5.9% to 21.6, Nvidia's options imply a ±7.2% move (≈$250B of market cap), and the equity risk premium is the thinnest since 2002. A 6% long bond eventually breaks something — housing, leveraged credit, or the equity multiple itself.

The asymmetry

Semiconductors carry ~11% of S&P 500 weight; a 7% Nvidia move is worth roughly 30 index points on its own before sympathy moves in AMD, Broadcom and the hyperscalers. Tonight we learn about the numerator (earnings). The denominator — the discount rate, sitting at 5.19% and climbing — is the part that should keep you up at night.
