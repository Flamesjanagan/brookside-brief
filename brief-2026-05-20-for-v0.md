## 1. The Morning Setup

Good morning, and welcome to the most consequential trading day of the quarter. After the closing bell tonight, Nvidia will report — and with it, deliver the single most important verdict the market will hear on whether the AI capital-expenditure supercycle is still accelerating or finally plateauing. The setup is electric: the stock has rebounded 1.6% pre-market to $224 and dragged the entire semiconductor complex up with it (Intel +4.8%, Marvell +4.7%, Micron +3.4%), snapping what had been a grim three-day slide.

That slide has a name, and it is the long bond. The 30-year Treasury yield hit 5.198% yesterday — its highest level since July 2007, nearly nineteen years. A Bank of America fund manager survey out this morning shows 62% of respondents now expect the 30-year to eventually reach 6%, a level last seen in 1999. The S&P 500 fell for a third straight session Tuesday, closing at 7,353.61, as the relentless climb in yields overwhelmed an otherwise calmer oil tape. This is the central tension of the moment: a still-humming earnings story colliding with a bond market that is rapidly repricing the entire cost of capital.

There is real news beyond Nvidia today. Powell speaks at 9:00 a.m. at the Atlanta Fed — his first remarks since the hot CPI/PPI prints. The Treasury sells $16 billion of 20-year bonds at 1:00 p.m., a genuine stress test of demand at these levels. And the retail read continues: Lowe's beat this morning, Target reports before the open. But make no mistake — everything today is a warm-up act for 4:20 p.m., when Jensen Huang steps to the microphone.

## 3. Top Stories

**Nvidia Reports Tonight — The AI Trade's Make-or-Break Moment.** At 4:20 p.m. ET, Nvidia delivers Q1 FY27 results, with the Street looking for $1.76 in EPS (up from $0.96 a year ago) on revenue of $79.04 billion — a 78% year-over-year jump. But the print is about guidance, not the quarter. Consensus for Q2 FY27 revenue sits near $87 billion; the bull case wants $90B-plus, and the whisper number for data-center revenue is $74 billion. The options market is pricing a ±7.2% move — roughly $250 billion of market cap in either direction. Nvidia rebounded 1.6% to $224 pre-market, leading a broad semiconductor bid (SMH +2.2%). A beat-and-raise likely reignites the year-end chase, while any guidance wobble — especially on China or gross margins — confirms the rotation out of megacap growth.

**30-Year Yield Hits 19-Year High — Fund Managers Eye 6%.** The long bond yield touched 5.198% Tuesday, its highest since July 2007, and is holding near 5.19% this morning. A BofA global fund manager survey found 62% expect the 30-year to eventually reach 6%. The 30-year fixed mortgage rate has climbed to 7.91%, the highest of the cycle, and the equity risk premium has compressed to its thinnest since 2002. Today's $16B 20-year auction at 1:00 p.m. is the immediate test.

**Lowe's Beats; Target on Deck — Consumer Read Stays Resilient.** Lowe's reported Q1 net sales of $23.1 billion, net income of $1.63 billion, and adjusted EPS of $3.03 — ahead of estimates. Comparable sales rose 0.6%, online up 15.5%. Management guided full-year adjusted EPS to $12.25–$12.75. Target reports before the open; the bar is low after a 22% YTD decline.

**Powell Speaks at 9:00 — First Remarks Since the Inflation Shock.** Chair Powell opens the Atlanta Fed's conference at 9:00 a.m., his first remarks since the April CPI (+3.8% Y/Y) and PPI (+6.0% Y/Y) prints. With the long bond at a 19-year high, Powell has every incentive to sound measured.

**Oil Stabilizes Near $102 as Iran Diplomacy Limps Forward.** WTI holds near $102 and Brent near $106, consolidating after Monday's drop on news Trump shelved a planned strike. The Strait of Hormuz remains partially restricted. A collapse in talks would put $115 Brent back in play within days.

## 4. Macro & Fed Watch

Two Fed events bracket the next 30 hours: Powell at 9:00 a.m. today, and the April 28–29 FOMC minutes tomorrow at 2:00 p.m. The minutes are the bigger event — revealing how deep the four-way dissent ran (Hammack and Logan pushed for a hike, Kashkari wanted optionality). ## 12. The Closing Thought

Markets love a clean narrative, and today they have an unusually pure one: a single company, reporting after a single bell, will tell us whether the defining investment theme of the decade is still intact. But the more important story may be the quieter one in the bond market, where the 30-year just hit a level not seen in nineteen years and fund managers are openly discussing 6%. If Nvidia delivers and the AI trade roars back, the celebration could be short-lived — because a 6% long bond eventually breaks something: housing, leveraged credit, or the equity multiple itself. Tonight we learn about the numerator. The denominator, at 5.19% and climbing, is the part that should keep you up at night. Trade it carefully.
